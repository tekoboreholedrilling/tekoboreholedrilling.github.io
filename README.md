The Borehole Drilling Process: A Step-by-Step Overview
Drilling a successful borehole requires meticulous planning, technical expertise, and adherence to regulatory standards. The process typically includes the following stages:

Hydrogeological Survey
A qualified hydrogeologist conducts a detailed assessment using geophysical methods to identify optimal drilling sites, estimate aquifer depth, and predict water yield. This step minimizes risks and ensures project feasibility.
Regulatory Approvals and Permits
Compliance with Kenyan law is mandatory. Applications are submitted to the Water Resources Authority (WRA) for authorization to construct and abstract water. An Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) approved by the National Environment Management Authority (NEMA) is also required to safeguard ecosystems.
Site Preparation and Mobilization
Equipment, including drilling rigs, is transported to the site. Safety protocols and environmental safeguards are established.
Drilling Operations
Using advanced rotary or percussion techniques (such as air or mud drilling), the borehole is sunk to the target depth. Casing—typically steel or PVC—is installed to prevent collapse and contamination, with screens placed in water-bearing zones.
Development and Test Pumping
The borehole undergoes development to clear debris, followed by 24-hour test pumping to measure sustainable yield, drawdown, and recovery rates. Water quality analysis confirms suitability for intended use.
Equipping and Completion
Submersible pumps (solar or electric), storage tanks, piping, and control systems are installed. Final grouting seals the borehole annulus, ensuring structural integrity.
